There is a skill that no design course puts at the top of the syllabus. It does not appear in job descriptions between "proficiency in Figma" and "experience with design systems." It has no shortcut in any tool.

But it is, without question, **the skill that separates designers who produce good work from designers who produce work that actually changes behaviour.**

That skill is empathy. And it is the most transferable thing I have ever carried from one discipline to another.

---

## Understanding Customers in Fashion

In fashion, empathy is not a soft skill. It is a survival skill.

A client walks into a fitting room and sits down. She tells you she wants something elegant for her sister's wedding. What she does not tell you — at least not in words — is that she has been self-conscious since having her second child, that she wants to feel like herself again, and that the last time she wore something she truly loved was four years ago.

> You are not dressing a body. You are dressing a feeling.

The designers I respected most were not the most technically skilled. They were the ones who could look at a client and understand — before a word was spoken — what she needed to feel. They noticed when someone touched a fabric twice before walking away. They heard the uncertainty behind "I think I like this one."

**Fashion taught me that people rarely ask for what they actually need.** They ask for what they think they are allowed to want, or what they can find the language for. The designer's job — the real job — is to close that gap. This required a particular kind of listening: not waiting for your turn to speak, but the kind that suspends your own preferences and makes space for someone else's reality to land.

## Understanding Users in Tech

When I moved into product design, the tools changed completely. The language changed. The deliverables changed. But the core requirement stayed exactly the same.

A user opens an app and hesitates at a form field. She rereads a label. She types something, deletes it, and types it again. What analytics records is a session with elevated completion time. What actually happened is that the language on that screen made her feel like she was doing something wrong.

> The hesitation is the data. Most designers never learn to read it.

In usability testing, I noticed the same thing I noticed in fitting rooms: **the most important information is always adjacent to what people say out loud.** A participant who says "I think I understand this" while scrolling back to reread the page does not understand it. A participant who says "it's fine" while visibly rushing does not think it's fine.

Empathy in tech means holding a user's entire context in mind — not just their task, but their day, their assumptions, their emotional state — and letting all of that inform every decision, from the size of a button to the tone of an error message. The confused form field was not a copy problem. **It was an empathy problem.** Someone designed that screen without ever truly inhabiting the perspective of the person who would use it.

---

## How Empathy Influences Decision-Making

Empathy does not just make you a better listener. It makes you a better decision-maker — because it changes what you treat as evidence.

Without it, you make decisions based on what seems logical from the inside. You use your team's language. You structure flows around internal processes. You design for the imaginary user who shares your context and your patience.

> When you design with empathy, you make decisions based on what is true from the outside.

**Empathy is what makes you fight for plain language** when the legal team wants clinical terminology. It is what makes you push back on a five-step onboarding flow when you know users are arriving already frustrated. It is what makes you insist on testing with real people before shipping — because you understand, viscerally, that your assumptions about the experience are not the experience.

Most design failures are empathy failures in disguise. The navigation nobody uses was designed by someone who understood the architecture but not the mental model of the person navigating it. The onboarding flow that loses 60% of users was designed by someone who had stopped being curious about the experience of arriving somewhere new.

---

Empathy is not a personality trait. It is a practice — something you have to actively choose in every brief, every research session, every design review.

My background in fashion gave me years of practice in the discipline of genuinely understanding another person's needs before making anything for them. That practice did not need to be translated when I moved into product design. It only needed to be applied to a different medium.

**The medium changes. The human on the other side does not.**

And the work — in fashion, in product design, in any discipline that exists to serve people — is always, at its core, the practice of seeing that human clearly enough to make something that fits.
