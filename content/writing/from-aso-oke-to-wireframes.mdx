The first time I sat in a product design critique, someone talked about "user pain points." I smiled — not because it was funny, but because I had heard that exact conversation a hundred times before. Just in Yoruba. Just in a fabric market in Ibadan. Just with a customer who needed a dress that hid her hips and celebrated her shoulders, and needed it before her daughter's introduction ceremony on Saturday.

> I had been solving user problems my whole career. I just did not have the vocabulary for it yet.

---

## The Brief Is the Brief

In fashion, a client walks in and sits down. Sometimes they know exactly what they want. More often, they know how they want to *feel* — powerful, soft, seen, elegant — and your job is to translate that feeling into a garment. You listen. You probe. You ask whether they are sitting or standing most of the evening. You ask about the lighting at the venue, because coral beads read differently under generator-powered yellow light than they do in daylight.

**This is user research.** It just happens over tea and fabric swatches instead of over Zoom with a discussion guide.

In product design, the brief arrives differently — a PRD, a Notion doc, a Slack message with three bullet points — but the underlying question is always the same: *what does this person actually need, and why?* Not what they asked for. What they need.

I learned the difference between those two things the hard way, in 2018, when a client asked for an all-white lace gown. She was a November bride in Osogbo. I made it. She wore it. She sweated through it by the second hour and spent the rest of her wedding day pulling the fabric away from her skin. She had told me what she wanted. I had not asked what she needed.

> After that, I always asked about the heat.

## Pattern-Making Is Information Architecture

Before a single stitch is placed, a pattern must exist. The pattern is the logic of the garment — where pieces meet, how they move together, what holds tension and what relaxes. A bad pattern produces a garment that fights the body wearing it. A good pattern produces something that feels inevitable, like it could not have been made any other way.

When I encountered information architecture for the first time — the practice of organizing content and navigation so that users can move through a product without thinking — I recognised it immediately. **It is pattern-making.** You are deciding where things meet, how they flow, what carries weight and what gives way.

The boning in a bodice is a navigation structure. The seam allowance is the padding between elements. The grain line — that invisible thread that determines how fabric hangs — is your visual hierarchy. Get it wrong and everything looks slightly off. Get it right and nobody notices, because it feels natural.

> Nobody compliments good information architecture. Nobody compliments a well-cut lining. That is how you know it is working.

## Toile First, Always

In fashion, before you cut the expensive fabric — the hand-woven aso-oke, the imported silk, the heavily embellished lace — you make a toile. A cheap cotton mock-up of the full garment, worn on the body, adjusted in real life. You find out where it pulls. You find out where it gaps. You add, you remove, you pin and re-pin until the shape is right. Then — and only then — you cut the real thing.

**Wireframes are toiles.**

**Prototypes are toiles.**

The entire "test early, fail cheap" philosophy that product design evangelises so loudly is something seamstresses in Lagos markets have practised for generations without naming it. You do not waste the good fabric on an untested idea. You build the rough version, you put it in front of a real person, and you watch what happens.

> I have watched a user struggle to find the checkout button the same way I have watched a bride struggle to reach her zip. Both are fixable. Both were avoidable. Both happen when you skip the toile.

---

## Finishing Is Half the Work

There is a saying among tailors that anyone can sew a dress, but only a skilled hand can finish one. Finishing — the hemming, the lining, the way button holes are cut, the weight of the zip — is invisible work. The customer does not see it. They feel it. A poorly lined garment feels cheap even when the outer fabric is expensive. A well-finished garment feels deliberate, considered, worth it.

In product design, this is the micro-interaction. The transition animation. The empty state that acknowledges you with warmth instead of a cold grey box. The error message written like a human being rather than a system log. **These things are not features. They are finishing.** And they are the difference between a product that users tolerate and one they return to.

> The work inside the work. That is where care lives.

---

## The Same Craft, Different Materials

I did not leave fashion to escape it. I brought it with me.

Every user interview I conduct carries the memory of sitting across from a client and truly listening — not for what she was saying, but for what she was trying to say. Every wireframe I sketch carries the muscle memory of pattern-making — building structure before beauty. Every prototype I put in front of a user carries the discipline of the toile — the willingness to be wrong before the work gets precious.

Fashion design taught me that the user's body is the final arbiter. **You can love your design. The dress still has to fit.**

Product design taught me the same lesson in a different room.

**The craft changes. The process does not.** And the best designers — in any discipline — are the ones who understand that the work is always, underneath everything, about the person on the other side of it.

> Mine just used to wear what I made.
