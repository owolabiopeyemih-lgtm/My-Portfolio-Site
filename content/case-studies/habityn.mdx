## The Problem

Most habit apps fail their users not because they are poorly designed — but because they are designed around perfection. Miss one day, and the streak breaks. The streak breaks, and motivation collapses. The app gets deleted.

> Habityn started from a single question: what if a habit app was designed around real human behaviour, not ideal human behaviour?

---

## Research & Discovery

### How people actually build habits

I spoke with people who had tried and abandoned multiple habit apps. The patterns were consistent:

- **The perfectionism trap**: users would miss one day, feel like they had failed, and stop entirely
- **Notification fatigue**: aggressive reminders trained users to ignore the app
- **Too many habits at once**: apps encouraged users to track everything, leading to overwhelm
- **No sense of progress**: a streak number is not the same as feeling like you are growing

The insight that shaped everything: **people don't need more accountability. They need more compassion.**

---

## Design Process

### The two-miss rule

Habityn's core mechanic is that a single missed day does not break a streak. Two consecutive misses do. This single decision — borrowed loosely from behavioural science research on relapse prevention — fundamentally changes the emotional relationship between user and app.

Users stay because the system forgives them the way a good coach does. Not endlessly, but enough.

### Onboarding

Most habit apps start with a list of habits to pick. Habityn starts with one question:

*"What's one thing you want to do every day?"*

One habit. One commitment. The research was clear: users who start with a single habit are significantly more likely to still be using an app three weeks later than users who set up five habits on day one.

### The reminder system

Instead of push notifications at fixed times, Habityn uses a preference-based nudge system:

- Users choose a *window* (morning, afternoon, evening), not a specific time
- Reminders only fire if the habit hasn't been logged yet
- After three consecutive completions without needing a reminder, reminders pause automatically

The logic: if a habit is working, the app should get out of the way.

### Weekly reflection

Every Sunday, Habityn surfaces a simple view: a heatmap of the past week, a one-line prompt ("What made this week easier or harder?"), and a forward-looking question ("What would make next week better?").

This is not journaling. It is a two-minute check-in designed to help users connect their behaviour to their experience — which is the only thing that makes habit change stick long-term.

---

## Visual Design

Habityn's visual language is calm and unhurried — deliberately unlike the high-energy, gamified aesthetic of most productivity apps. Soft backgrounds, generous whitespace, and muted tones that feel restful rather than urgent.

**The streak display** does not use fire emojis or bold numbers. It uses a quiet arc — a partial circle that fills over time. The metaphor is growth, not a score.

**Colours** shift subtly with time of day. Morning sessions have a cooler, energising palette. Evening sessions warm to amber tones. The app feels alive without demanding attention.

---

## Outcome

> Habits are not built through willpower. They are built through systems that make showing up easier than not showing up.

Habityn is designed to be that system — quiet, forgiving, and built around the reality that consistency matters more than perfection, and that the best habit app is one you actually keep using.
