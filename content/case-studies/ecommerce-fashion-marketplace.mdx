## Overview

Independent fashion designers consistently struggle to reach customers beyond their immediate networks. Existing e-commerce platforms are built for general retail and require significant technical setup - creating a barrier for creative entrepreneurs whose time and energy goes into making, not managing software.

This personal project explores what a fashion marketplace built specifically for independent creators might look like. It draws directly on my six years as Lead Fashion Designer at G-Touch Apparels in Nigeria, where I saw firsthand how talented designers lacked the tools to scale their reach.

## The Problem

The core tension for independent fashion sellers is time. They are designers, makers, and business owners simultaneously. A marketplace that requires hours of setup, technical knowledge, or complex inventory management actively works against them.

For buyers, the problem is discovery. Finding independent designers currently requires navigating social media, marketplaces not built for fashion, or word of mouth. There is no single destination where quality independent fashion is curated and easy to browse.

## My Role

This was a solo personal project. I owned the full process - problem definition, research, information architecture, visual design, and prototyping. The goal was to build a portfolio piece that demonstrated end-to-end product thinking and drew on my domain expertise in fashion.

## Research

**Seller Perspective - Personal Experience**

My six years in the fashion industry gave me a deep understanding of the seller's workflow. I knew the language of the industry, the seasonal rhythm of collections, the complexity of made-to-order versus ready-to-wear, and the frustration of platforms that treated fashion like any other product category.

I supplemented this with informal interviews with three independent designers in my network, focusing on what tools they currently used to sell online and what their biggest frustrations were.

**Buyer Research**

I interviewed five fashion buyers - people who regularly purchased clothing from independent brands. Key themes that emerged:

- Discovery was the biggest challenge; buyers relied heavily on Instagram and personal referrals
- Trust was a barrier when buying from unfamiliar designers - buyers wanted to see more than product photos
- Sizing and quality information was consistently lacking on existing platforms

**Competitive Audit**

I reviewed Depop, ASOS Marketplace, Etsy, and Instagram Shopping. Each had significant gaps when evaluated against the needs of independent fashion designers and their buyers. No platform offered a fashion-first experience with tools built around the designer's actual workflow.

## Design Decisions

**Mobile First**

The primary use case for both buyers and sellers is mobile. Buyers browse on their phones. Sellers photograph and upload products on their phones. Every screen was designed mobile-first, with desktop as a secondary consideration.

**Discovery Over Search**

The buyer home screen leads with editorial-style discovery - curated lookbooks, featured designers, and trending pieces - rather than a search bar. Search exists, but the experience is optimised for browsing and serendipitous discovery, which is how fashion buyers actually shop.

**Seller Onboarding in Familiar Language**

The seller onboarding flow was written in the language of fashion, not e-commerce. "Add a collection" instead of "create a product category". "Lookbook" instead of "image gallery". Small language decisions that made the experience feel built for designers rather than adapted for them.

**Trust Signals**

Each designer profile included a short story section, a process video slot, and visible buyer reviews - giving buyers the context they needed to purchase from unfamiliar names with confidence.

## Prototype and Testing

I built a high-fidelity interactive prototype in Framer covering the buyer discovery flow and the seller product upload flow. I tested the prototype with two buyers and two sellers, validating the core journeys and identifying one friction point in the checkout flow that I revised before completing the project.

## Reflection

This project reinforced how much domain expertise can shape design decisions. Having worked in fashion gave me an instinct for what felt right - in the language, the visual hierarchy, the flow - that I could not have developed purely from research. It also gave me the confidence to make opinionated design choices rather than defaulting to convention.
