## The Problem

Every developer has been there: you want to build something, you open a blank code editor, and then you stare at it for forty minutes. Not because you lack skill — but because choosing *what* to build is its own problem, and nobody teaches you how to solve it.

The gap between "I want to improve" and "I know what to build next" is wide, and most solutions — GitHub trending, dev blogs, Twitter threads — are either too generic or too advanced. A junior developer looking at a senior engineer's project list feels overwhelmed. A senior engineer looking at beginner tutorials feels bored.

**The insight: project inspiration fails because it is not personalised.**

---

## Research & Discovery

### Who is stuck?

I spoke with developers across three experience levels — people just starting out, mid-level engineers looking to stretch, and senior developers who wanted complex architectural challenges. The pattern was consistent across all three:

> "I know I need to build more projects. I just don't know which ones are worth my time."

The blockers differed by level:

- **Beginners** felt overwhelmed by scope — they didn't know if an idea was too hard or too easy
- **Intermediate developers** wanted projects that would actually push them into new territory
- **Senior engineers** were bored by typical project lists — they wanted genuine complexity

### What was already out there?

Existing solutions had one of two problems:

1. **Too broad** — generic "build a todo app" lists that applied to no one specifically
2. **Too narrow** — tutorials that locked you into one tech stack or one approach

Neither solution respected where the developer actually was in their journey.

---

## Design Process

### Defining the core interaction

The central design question was: **how do you personalise without friction?**

Most personalisation systems require sign-up, profiles, questionnaires. That creates a barrier before the user has even seen value. The goal was to make personalisation feel immediate — answer two or three questions, get something useful. No account. No onboarding flow.

The three inputs I settled on:

1. **Skill level** — three clear tiers with honest descriptions of what each means in practice
2. **Interest domains** — multi-select categories so users could combine interests naturally (e.g. Frontend + AI/ML)
3. **Volume** — how many ideas at once (1 to 5), because some people want one great idea, others want a shortlist to choose from

### Information architecture

The flow is deliberately linear and short:

```
Select skill level → Choose domains → Set volume → Generate
```

No back-and-forth. No progressive disclosure that confuses. The page has one job and it does it without ceremony.

### The "Surprise Me" mode

One research insight that shaped the design: some developers don't want a filtered result — they want to be pushed outside their comfort zone. The "Surprise Me" option bypasses all filters and generates a random idea. This serves a different mental mode — exploration instead of targeting.

Having both modes on the same screen without either competing for attention required careful layout work. The primary CTA is "Generate Ideas." "Surprise Me" lives beside it — same visual weight, clearly secondary intent.

---

## Visual Design

The interface is intentionally minimal. Developer tools that try too hard to look "cool" end up feeling distracting. The priority was:

- **Clarity of inputs** — every control should be immediately understandable with zero explanation
- **Fast feedback** — selected states, hover states, and generated results all respond without lag
- **No clutter** — nothing on screen that doesn't serve the task

The skill tier cards use a three-column layout that makes comparison easy. Each tier has a short label and a one-line description of who it is for — not what it includes, but *who it is for*. That distinction matters: it orients the user relationally, not technically.

Domain tags are multi-select chips — visual, tappable, easy to scan. The selected state is clear. Deselecting is as easy as selecting.

---

## Outcome

ForgeFlow compresses what used to be a 30-minute aimless browsing session into a 30-second intentional interaction. Developers leave with something concrete — not just an idea, but an idea that fits where they actually are.

The product validates a principle that runs through all good tool design: **the best tools respect your time, your level, and your goal — and get out of the way.**

> Developers don't have a motivation problem. They have a direction problem. ForgeFlow solves direction.
