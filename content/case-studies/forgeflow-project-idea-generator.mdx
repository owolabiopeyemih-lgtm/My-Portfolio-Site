## The Problem

Every developer has been there: you want to build something, you open a blank code editor, and then you stare at it for forty minutes. Not because you lack skill — but because **choosing what to build is its own problem**, and nobody teaches you how to solve it.

The gap between *"I want to improve"* and *"I know what to build next"* is wide. Most solutions — GitHub trending, dev blogs, tutorial lists — are either too generic or too advanced. A junior developer looking at a senior engineer's project list feels overwhelmed. A senior engineer scanning beginner tutorials feels bored.

> Project inspiration fails because it is not personalised. ForgeFlow was built to fix that.

---

## Research & Discovery

### Who is stuck?

I spoke with developers across three experience levels — people just starting out, mid-level engineers looking to stretch, and senior developers who wanted complex architectural challenges. The pattern was consistent across all three groups:

> "I know I need to build more projects. I just don't know which ones are worth my time."

The **blockers differed significantly by level:**

- **Beginners** felt overwhelmed by scope — they couldn't tell if an idea was too hard or too easy for where they actually were
- **Intermediate developers** wanted projects that would push them into genuinely new territory, not just reinforce what they already knew
- **Senior engineers** were bored by typical project lists — they wanted architectural complexity and real-world constraints to work against

### What was already out there?

Existing solutions had one of two fundamental problems:

**Too broad** — generic "build a todo app" lists that applied to no one specifically. The advice was sound but the fit was wrong.

**Too narrow** — tutorials that locked you into a single tech stack or a single approach, leaving no room for the developer's own context or interests.

Neither solution respected where the developer actually was in their journey. That was the gap ForgeFlow was designed to fill.

---

## Design Process

### Defining the core interaction

The central design question was: **how do you personalise without adding friction?**

Most personalisation systems require sign-up, a profile, or a questionnaire before you see any value. That creates a barrier before the user has even had a reason to trust the product. The goal was to make personalisation feel *immediate* — answer two or three questions, get something genuinely useful. No account. No onboarding flow.

The three inputs I settled on:

1. **Skill level** — three clear tiers with honest, plain-language descriptions of what each means in practice
2. **Interest domains** — multi-select categories so users could combine interests naturally (e.g. Frontend + AI/ML, or Backend + Security)
3. **Volume** — how many ideas to generate at once (1 to 5), because some people want one great idea and others want a shortlist to choose from

### Keeping the flow short

The interaction is deliberately linear and fast:

**Choose skill level → Select domains → Set volume → Generate**

No back-and-forth. No progressive disclosure that adds confusion. The page has one job and it does it without ceremony. Every step reinforces the same promise: *this will be quick and you will leave with something useful.*

### The "Surprise Me" mode

One research insight that genuinely shaped the design: **some developers don't want a filtered result** — they want to be pushed outside their comfort zone entirely. The "Surprise Me" option bypasses all filters and generates a random idea across any skill level or domain.

This serves a different mental mode — exploration instead of targeting. Having both modes on the same screen without either competing for attention required careful layout work. The primary CTA is **Generate Ideas**. "Surprise Me" sits beside it — clearly secondary in intent, but equal in accessibility.

---

## Visual Design

The interface is intentionally minimal. **Developer tools that try too hard to look impressive end up feeling distracting.** The design priorities were:

- **Clarity of inputs** — every control should be immediately understandable with zero explanation needed
- **Fast feedback** — selected states, hover states, and generated results all respond without lag
- **No clutter** — nothing on screen that does not directly serve the task

The **skill tier cards** use a three-column layout that makes comparison effortless. Each tier carries a short label and a one-line description of *who it is for* — not what it includes, but who it speaks to. That distinction matters: it orients the user relationally, not technically. A developer should be able to read a tier and immediately know whether it describes them.

**Domain tags** are multi-select chips — visual, tappable, easy to scan at a glance. The selected state is high-contrast and unmissable. Deselecting is as frictionless as selecting.

> The best developer tools feel like they were built by someone who understood the frustration, not just the feature request.

---

## Outcome

ForgeFlow compresses what used to be a thirty-minute aimless browsing session into a thirty-second intentional interaction. Developers leave with something concrete — not just an idea, but **an idea that fits where they actually are** in their journey.

The product validates a principle that runs through all good tool design: respect the user's time, respect their level, respect their goal — then get out of the way entirely.

> Developers don't have a motivation problem. They have a direction problem. ForgeFlow solves direction.
