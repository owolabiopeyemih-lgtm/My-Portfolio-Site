## Overview

Medication non-adherence is one of the most persistent and costly problems in healthcare. Patients miss doses, confuse schedules, or simply lose track when managing multiple medications across different times of day. This personal project explores how a well-designed mobile app can meaningfully support patients in building consistent medication habits - particularly those managing complex or long-term treatment plans.

The project was informed directly by my experience leading teams at Bolff Health Care in the UK, where patient medication management and adherence were part of daily operational reality.

## The Problem

Existing reminder apps treat medication management like a to-do list. A checkbox and a notification. That approach misses the reality of patients managing multiple medications with different dosages, frequencies, and instructions - particularly older adults, patients with chronic conditions, or those in post-operative recovery.

The emotional dimension also goes unaddressed. Managing a complex medication schedule can be anxiety-inducing. The design of most apps amplifies that anxiety rather than reducing it - dense lists, clinical language, and punishing UX when a dose is missed.

There was also a clear gap around caregiver support. Family members and care workers often need visibility into a patient's adherence without being given full control of their account.

## My Role

This was a solo personal project built to demonstrate healthcare UX thinking and draw on my operational background at Bolff Health Care. I led all research, design, and testing.

## Research

**Healthcare Context - Personal Experience**

Three years leading multidisciplinary teams at Bolff Health Care gave me direct exposure to the operational challenges of medication management - the gaps in communication between patients and care staff, the consequences of missed doses, and the varying levels of health literacy among patients we supported.

**User Interviews**

I conducted interviews with four people who managed ongoing medication regimens and two healthcare workers who support patients with medication management. Key findings:

- Patients with multiple medications described feeling overwhelmed by the complexity of their schedule
- Missing a dose triggered significant anxiety, particularly for patients on time-sensitive medications
- Caregivers had no reliable way to monitor adherence remotely without calling or visiting
- Health literacy varied significantly - medical terminology in existing apps created confusion
- Most participants used phone alarm systems rather than dedicated apps, suggesting the current options were not meeting their needs

## Design Principles

Based on the research, I established three guiding principles for the design:

1. **Clarity over completeness** - show patients only what they need at the moment they need it, not everything at once
2. **Reassurance over judgment** - when doses are missed, the app responds with support rather than alarm
3. **Accessibility by default** - design for the most constrained user; every other user benefits

## Design Decisions

**Daily Timeline View**

Rather than a list of medications, the home screen presents a daily timeline - a horizontal view of the day broken into morning, afternoon, evening, and night slots. Medications appear in their slots with clear labels, dosage information, and a single tap to mark as taken. The visual metaphor of a timeline feels more natural than a checkbox list and is easier to scan at a glance.

**Caregiver Sharing**

The caregiver sharing feature allows patients to share a read-only view of their schedule and adherence history with a named family member or care worker, via a simple link sent by SMS or email. Caregivers can see what has been taken and what is outstanding without needing to log in or create an account. The patient retains full control and can revoke access at any time.

**Missed Dose Handling**

When a dose window passes without being marked, the app sends a gentle follow-up notification and marks the slot as missed in a neutral colour - not red, which tests showed felt alarming. A missed dose prompts a simple "What happened?" screen with three options - missed intentionally, forgot, or felt unwell - feeding into the adherence history without judgment.

**Accessibility**

Every element was designed to meet WCAG 2.1 AA standards as a baseline. Touch targets are a minimum of 48x48px. Body text is set at 16px minimum. Colour is never the sole conveyor of information. The type scale uses high contrast ratios throughout. I also designed a high-contrast mode for users with low vision.

## Usability Testing

I tested the prototype with three participants - two patients and one caregiver. Key findings:

- The timeline view was immediately understood by all three participants
- The caregiver sharing setup was completed without assistance in under two minutes
- One participant initially missed the "mark as taken" interaction; I revised the tap target size and added a subtle animation to draw attention

## Reflection

This project reinforced a conviction I brought from healthcare work: in high-stakes, emotionally loaded contexts, restraint in design is not a limitation - it is the work. The most meaningful design decisions were about what to leave out, what language to use, and how to make an already stressed user feel supported rather than monitored.
