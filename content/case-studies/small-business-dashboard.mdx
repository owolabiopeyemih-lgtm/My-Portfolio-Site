## Overview

Small business owners in service-based industries - salons, repair shops, cleaning services, tutors - routinely manage bookings, staff schedules, and service catalogues across a patchwork of tools. This project, completed during my product design internship at Podus, set out to consolidate those fragmented workflows into a single, purpose-built dashboard.

## The Problem

Through initial discovery conversations with owners, a clear pattern emerged: the average owner was switching between three to five separate tools daily - a spreadsheet for staff rota, a messaging app for booking confirmations, a notes app for service pricing, and a separate calendar for scheduling. There was no single source of truth, which led to double bookings, missed appointments, and a constant feeling of operational chaos.

The brief was to design a dashboard that brought these workflows together without overwhelming users who had no technical background and limited time to learn a new system.

## My Role

As Product Design Intern at Podus, I was involved in the full design process - from shaping the research questions through to final high-fidelity delivery and design handoff. I worked closely with a senior designer and product manager, contributing to every stage independently while receiving regular feedback and direction.

## Research

**User Interviews**

I conducted five semi-structured interviews with small business owners across different service categories. The interviews focused on understanding their daily workflows, the tools they currently used, what they found most stressful, and how they defined a "good day" operationally.

Key findings:

- All five owners described their booking process as a source of stress, particularly around last-minute changes and no-shows
- Staff availability tracking was universally handled informally - through text messages or verbal check-ins
- None of the participants had a clear view of their business performance at a glance
- Owners wanted to spend less time managing operations and more time delivering their service

**Competitive Audit**

I reviewed four existing tools in the market. Most were feature-heavy and built for larger businesses, with complex onboarding and terminology that felt unfamiliar to independent owners. The opportunity was a simpler, more opinionated experience designed specifically for the solo or small-team operator.

## Information Architecture

Based on the research findings, I structured the dashboard around three core modules:

1. **Bookings** - the daily schedule, upcoming appointments, and a quick-add flow for new bookings
2. **Staff** - availability at a glance, shift assignment, and basic profile management
3. **Services** - the catalogue of what the business offers, with pricing and duration

A persistent sidebar with clear iconography gave quick access between modules. The default landing view was the day's schedule - the thing every owner checked first thing in the morning.

## Design Process

I started with paper sketches to explore layout options before moving into low-fidelity wireframes in Figma. After two rounds of wireframe feedback with the product manager and senior designer, I moved into high-fidelity designs.

The visual design prioritised clarity over complexity - a clean typographic hierarchy, generous spacing, and a muted colour palette with a single accent colour used only for actionable items and alerts. The goal was a UI that felt calm and in control, reflecting how we wanted owners to feel using it.

## Usability Testing

I ran three moderated usability sessions using Maze, presenting participants with task scenarios: add a new booking, check staff availability for tomorrow, and update a service price. All three participants completed each task without assistance. The feedback highlighted one point of confusion in the booking creation flow, which I revised before final handoff.

## Outcome

The final designs covered the full dashboard experience across desktop and tablet viewpoints, including empty states, error states, and a light/dark mode variant. I also produced a component library in Figma to support the engineering team in building consistently.

The project gave me hands-on experience running research, iterating based on feedback, and working within a real product team for the first time as a product designer.
